# **1. Precision weak‑field tests (Solar System, binary pulsars)**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
These are the most over‑constrained gravitational tests in existence.  
If PM deviates even slightly from GR here, it’s dead.

### What to compute  
- Light deflection at small angles  
- Shapiro delay  
- Perihelion precession  
- Frame dragging (LAGEOS, Gravity Probe B)  
- Orbital decay in binary pulsars  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
Weak‑field PM *looks* like GR, but it’s not identical.  
If the refractive‑index mapping or the flow response introduces even tiny deviations, they will show up here.

### What would kill PM  
A mismatch at the \(10^{-5}\)–\(10^{-6}\) level in any of these.

---

# **2. Galaxy–scale lensing profiles**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
Weak lensing is measured statistically across millions of galaxies.  
It’s extremely hard for a non‑GR theory to match both:

- rotation curves  
- and lensing shear  

with the same parameter set.

### What to compute  
- PM lensing convergence maps  
- PM shear profiles  
- PM rotation curves  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM predicts the right rotation curves but the wrong lensing shear, that’s a fatal inconsistency.

### What would kill PM  
A systematic mismatch in the shear–mass relation.

---

# **3. Compact‑object mass–radius curves**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
NICER and X‑ray burst data give constraints on neutron‑star radii.  
PM predicts a **maximum density** and therefore a **maximum compactness**.

### What to compute  
- PM mass–radius curves  
- PM surface redshift as a function of mass  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM predicts radii that are too small or too large for observed neutron stars, it’s done.

### What would kill PM  
A neutron star with \(z > 2\) or \(R < 2.5GM/c^2\).

---

# **4. EHT shadow structure**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
PM predicts:

- no event horizon  
- a soft shadow  
- finite interior flux  
- slightly different photon‑sphere behaviour

EHT data is not perfect, but it’s good enough to constrain some of this.

### What to compute  
- PM ray‑tracing images  
- PM visibility amplitudes  
- PM shadow edge sharpness  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM predicts too much interior flux or the wrong shadow size, it’s falsified.

### What would kill PM  
A shadow radius inconsistent with EHT by more than ~10%.

---

# **5. Gravitational‑wave ringdown**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
PM predicts:

- no horizon QNMs  
- possible surface modes  
- possible echoes  

LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA can already constrain some of this.

### What to compute  
- PM post‑merger oscillation spectrum  
- PM echo timescales  
- PM damping rates  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM predicts echoes that aren’t seen, or fails to reproduce the observed ringdown frequencies, that’s a problem.

### What would kill PM  
A high‑SNR ringdown event matching GR’s horizon QNMs with no room for PM deviations.

---

# **6. Cosmological perturbations**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
This is where many alternative gravity theories die.

### What to compute  
- PM linear perturbation growth  
- PM matter power spectrum  
- PM CMB lensing  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM predicts the wrong growth rate of structure, it’s falsified.

### What would kill PM  
A mismatch with the observed \(f\sigma_8(z)\) curve.

---

# **7. Energy bookkeeping in collapse**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
PM predicts:

- no singularities  
- a radiation‑dominated core  
- a maximum compression

But collapse must conserve energy and momentum.

### What to compute  
- PM collapse simulations  
- energy flux into the core  
- stability of the uniform high‑energy state  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If energy piles up in a way that violates conservation or produces runaway behaviour, PM breaks.

### What would kill PM  
A collapse solution that cannot reach equilibrium without violating conservation.

---

# **8. Rotating configurations**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
Rotation is where many medium‑based theories fail.

### What to compute  
- PM rotating compact objects  
- PM frame‑dragging analogue  
- PM stability under rotation  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM cannot support rotation without tearing itself apart, it’s done.

### What would kill PM  
A maximum spin far below observed millisecond pulsars.

---

# **9. Electromagnetic coupling**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
PM currently treats EM as propagating in the medium but not interacting with the medium’s dynamics.

### What to compute  
- PM refractive corrections to EM propagation  
- PM predictions for pulsar timing  
- PM predictions for FRB dispersion  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If PM predicts dispersion or birefringence that isn’t observed, it’s falsified.

### What would kill PM  
Any frequency‑dependent speed of light inconsistent with constraints.

---

# **10. Internal consistency checks**  
### Why this is a good attack surface  
Even if PM matches observations, it must also be mathematically consistent.

### What to compute  
- hyperbolicity of the PDEs  
- energy positivity  
- well‑posedness of initial value problems  
- absence of superluminal modes  

### Why this is dangerous for PM  
If the equations are ill‑posed or allow runaway modes, PM collapses internally.

### What would kill PM  
A superluminal characteristic speed or loss of hyperbolicity.
