\section{Physical Picture}
%====================================================================

\textbf{The medium.}
Pushing Medium (PM) proposes that all of space is filled by a physical
elastic medium, much as a gas fills a vessel.  In its undisturbed state the
medium is uniform, with a reference density $\rho_{\mathrm{nuc}}$.  A mass
\emph{compresses} the surrounding medium, raising the local density above
$\rho_{\mathrm{nuc}}$.  That compression is described by a single scalar
field $\phi(\mathbf{r},t)$.

\textbf{The compression field $\phi$ and refractive index $n$.}
The local compression state is encoded as:
\[
  n(\mathbf{r}) = e^{\phi(\mathbf{r})}, \qquad \phi = \ln n \geq 0
\]
where $n$ is the refractive index of the medium at that point, and $\phi = 0$
($n=1$) is uncompressed free space.  Near a mass $M$:
\[
  \phi(r) = \frac{2GM}{c^2 r}
\]
so $\phi$ and $n$ are both largest close to the mass and fall off at infinity.
\textbf{$\phi$ is the PM analog of GR spacetime curvature}: where GR says
``spacetime curves near a mass'', PM says ``the medium becomes more compressed
(denser) near a mass''.

\textbf{Gravity from compression gradients.}
A gradient $\nabla\phi \neq 0$ means the medium is more compressed in one
direction.  This acts like a pressure gradient: it pushes everything toward
the region of higher density.  For a massive (slow) particle:
\[
  \mathbf{a} = \tfrac{c^2}{2}\,\nabla\phi
  \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
  \mathbf{a} = -\frac{GM}{r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}} \quad \text{(Newtonian gravity)}
\]

\textbf{Lensing: why light bends twice as much as a thrown ball.}
Light is refracted by the medium.  A denser region ($n > 1$) slows light
locally, causing wavefronts to tip toward it â€” exactly Fermat's principle.
Light couples to \emph{both} the time-stretching and space-compression of
the effective metric (both $g_{tt}$ and $g_{rr}$), giving a deflection
\[
  \alpha_{\mathrm{light}} = \frac{4GM}{c^2 b}
\]
A massive slow particle is governed only by the time component ($g_{tt}$,
i.e.\ kinetic-energy vs.\ potential-energy balance), giving acceleration
$c^2/2$ per unit $\nabla\phi$ instead of $c^2$.  The ratio is exactly
$\mathbf{2:1}$ â€” the origin of the factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$ in the force law.

\textbf{The flow field $\mathbf{u}$ and frame drag.}
A moving or rotating mass also \emph{drags} the medium, inducing a vector
flow field $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{r},t)$ in addition to the scalar compression
$\phi$.  This flow Fizeau-drags light paths â€” a ray travelling with
$\mathbf{u}$ is swept along, one travelling against it is retarded â€” and
modifies the orbital motion of massive bodies.  This is the PM mechanism
corresponding to frame-dragging in GR.

\textbf{What matter is.}
Matter is not a separate substance inserted into the medium.  It is a
\emph{stable, localised configuration} of the medium itself â€” a region where
the medium is held in a distinct compressed state by internal pressure
balance.  The boundary of a material body is where its internal compressed
state meets the ambient uncompressed medium ($\phi \to 0$).  This is why the
source term $\rho$ in the Poisson equation is not an independent input: it is
\emph{the medium's own compressed state} acting as its own source.  The
density law $\rho = \rho_{\mathrm{nuc}}\,e^{\phi}$ expresses exactly this
self-sourcing â€” the more compressed the medium, the more it sources further
compression in the surrounding region.

\textbf{What energy is: two phases of the medium.}
The medium has two distinct regimes:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item \textbf{Matter phase} ($0 \leq \phi < 1$): localised, structured,
        clumping configurations.  The deformation energy $U(\phi)$ stored in
        this configuration is what we call rest-mass energy.  Changing the
        configuration (e.g.\ nuclear decay, fusion) changes the stored energy
        â€” the excess is released as radiation.  This is the PM account of
        $E = mc^2$: matter and energy are different organisational states of
        the same medium.
  \item \textbf{Energy phase} ($\phi \to 1$, stability limit): the medium
        can no longer sustain a localised matter-phase structure.  At $\phi=1$
        the restoring force vanishes ($U''(1) = 0$) and the medium transitions
        to a uniform, non-clumping state.  This is the only strong-field limit
        in PM â€” not a singularity, but a phase transition.
\end{itemize}
The stability cap $\phi < 1$ is therefore not an arbitrary cut-off: it is
where the matter phase becomes mechanically unstable.

\subsection*{Diagnostic: Three-Way Disconnection of $U(\phi)$}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------

The deformation energy $U(\phi)$ is currently used \emph{only} as a
stability condition.  Three structural gaps exist.

\textbf{Gap~1 â€” $U(\phi)$ is absent from the Lagrangian.}
The PM Lagrangian density (see Â§PM Lagrangian) contains gradient and
kinetic terms $-\tfrac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2 + \tfrac{(\partial_t\phi)^2}{2c_\phi^2}$
but no potential term $-V(\phi)$.  Consequently the static equation of
motion for $\phi$ is the linear Poisson equation
\[
  \nabla^2\phi = -\tfrac{8\pi G}{c^2}\rho_M,
\]
with \emph{no self-coupling from $U$}.  The stability cap $\phi<1$ is
therefore an externally imposed condition, not a dynamical consequence
of the Lagrangian.  If $U$ were included as $-V(\phi)=-U(\phi)/c_\phi^2$,
the field equation would become
\[
  \nabla^2\phi \;-\; \frac{U'(\phi)}{c_\phi^2}
  = -\tfrac{8\pi G}{c^2}\rho_M
  \qquad\bigl[U'(\phi)=\varepsilon_0(2\phi-\phi^2)\bigr],
\]
a nonlinear Poisson equation that
would self-regulate compression and make $\phi_{\rm crit}=1$ an emergent
feature rather than a postulate.

\textbf{Gap~2 â€” $U(\phi)$ and the EOS are thermodynamically inconsistent.}
If $U(\phi)$ were the fluid's internal energy density, the thermodynamic
pressure would follow from $P_{\rm thermo}=\rho^2\partial(U/\rho)/\partial\rho$.
Evaluating with $\rho=\rho_{\rm nuc}e^{\phi}$:
\[
  P_{\rm thermo}
  = \varepsilon_0\!\left(2\phi - 2\phi^2 + \frac{\phi^3}{3}\right).
\]
At small $\phi$ this gives $P_{\rm thermo}\approx 2\varepsilon_0\phi$, whereas
the stated EOS gives $P_{\rm EOS}=\tfrac{c^2}{2}(\rho-\rho_{\rm nuc})
\approx\tfrac{\varepsilon_0}{2}\phi$.
The ratio $P_{\rm thermo}/P_{\rm EOS}\approx 4$ at lowest order.
They are measuring fundamentally different things and are never
reconciled in the current framework.  $U(\phi)$ is thus \emph{not}
the thermodynamic free energy of the PM fluid â€” its physical
interpretation is unspecified.

\textbf{Gap~3 â€” Field gradient energy $\tfrac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2$
does not gravitate.}
The Hamiltonian (see Â§Hamiltonian) contains $\tfrac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2$
as positive-definite field energy, yet the Poisson equation sources
$\phi$ from $\rho_M$ alone.  In the strong-field interior:
\[
  |\nabla\phi|^2 \sim \left(\frac{\mu_G m(r)}{r^2}\right)^2.
\]
At the PM stability cap this omitted self-energy density is
$\lesssim 1\%$ of $\rho_{\rm crit}c^2$, so it is negligible for
stellar structure â€” but it is a formal inconsistency that would
become important if $U(\phi)$ is modified to allow higher compactness.

\textbf{Root cause.}  All three gaps trace to the same origin:
$U(\phi)$ was introduced as a phenomenological stability condition
(analogous to a Landau order parameter) without being derived from
the PM Lagrangian.  Because it feeds into neither the Poisson
equation nor the hydrostatic equilibrium, the compact-star sector
rests on a stability criterion that is decoupled from the dynamics.
This is the most likely site of the strong-field aberration.

\textbf{Minimal repair strategy.}
A self-consistent extension requires choosing \emph{one} of:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(a)] Add $-U(\phi)/c_\phi^2$ to the Lagrangian; re-derive the EOS
        from the modified equation of motion; accept that
        $P_{\rm EOS}$ changes.
  \item[(b)] Keep the EOS and the Lagrangian as-is; replace $U(\phi)$ with
        a different stability argument whose critical point $\phi_c$
        is a free parameter fit to compact-object data (effectively
        relaxing the hard cap).
  \item[(c)] Interpret $U(\phi)$ as an \emph{additional} elastic pressure
        of the medium, add it to the hydrostatic equilibrium
        ($P_{\rm total}=P_{\rm EOS}+U'(\phi)\times$(geometric factor)),
        and require thermodynamic self-consistency.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{GW Polarisation: Scalar Breathing Mode}
%====================================================================

\noindent\textbf{Question:} Does the PM scalar field $n(\mathbf{r},t)$ produce a
breathing-mode (scalar, $l{=}0$) GW polarisation during binary inspiral, in
violation of the LIGO~O3 bound?

\noindent\textbf{Conservation-law proof (no leading-order breathing radiation):}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Monopole:} total mass $M_{\rm tot} = \text{const}
    \;\Rightarrow\;$ no monopole scalar radiation.
  \item \textbf{Dipole:} centre-of-mass fixed for an isolated binary
    $\;\Rightarrow\;$ no dipole scalar radiation.
  \item \textbf{Scalar quadrupole:} for a \emph{circular} orbit the scalar
    trace of the mass quadrupole
    \[
      I_{\rm trace} = \sum_i m_i \, |\mathbf{r}_i|^2 = \mu a^2 = \text{const}
    \]
    is \emph{constant} (unlike the STF tensor $\tilde I_{ij}$ which oscillates
    at $2\omega$). Hence $\ddot{I}_{\rm trace} = 0$ exactly, and there is
    \textbf{zero leading-order scalar quadrupole radiation}.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{First non-zero breathing contribution} (from orbital decay):
\[
  \ddot{I}_{\rm trace} = -\frac{4\mu\beta^2}{a^6}, \qquad
  \beta = \frac{64}{5}\frac{G^3 M_1 M_2 M_{\rm tot}}{c^5}
\]
Ratio of breathing strain to tensor $h_+$:
\[
  \frac{|h_b|}{|h_+|} \;\approx\; \frac{\beta^2}{\omega^2 a^8}
  \;\sim\; \left(\frac{v}{c}\right)^{10} \times (\text{mass factor})
\]

\subsection*{PM Structure-Growth Scenarios}

\textbf{Scenario 1 --- PM-Static} (density perturbation in
$H_{\rm PM}$ background with no friction term from the drag force):
\begin{align*}
  \ddot\delta &= 4\pi G\rho_m\,\delta
  \;\Longrightarrow\; \delta(t)\propto e^{\Gamma t}, \\
  \Gamma &= \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}}\,H_0\sqrt{\Omega_m}
  \approx 0.687\,H_0
\end{align*}
The growth rate observable $f_{\rm static}(z) = \Gamma / H_{\rm PM}(z)$
\emph{increases with redshift} (opposite to $\Lambda$CDM), grossly over-predicting
cluster abundances.

\textbf{Scenario 2 --- PM-Drag} (index decay provides Hubble-like friction):
\begin{align*}
  D'' &+ \frac{3-\beta}{a}\,D' - \frac{3\Omega_{\rm eff}}{2\,a^{5-2\beta}}\,D = 0
\end{align*}
where primes denote $d/da$ and $E(a)=a^{-\beta}$.  The characteristic
power-law growing mode $D\propto a^p$ satisfies
\begin{align*}
  p^2 + (2-\beta)\,p - \tfrac{3}{2}\Omega_{\rm eff} &= 0 \\
  p &= \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[(\beta-2)+\sqrt{(2-\beta)^2+6\Omega_{\rm eff}}\right].
\end{align*}
For $\beta=\tfrac{3}{2}$ ($\Lambda$CDM matter-domination), $p=1$ exactly.

\textbf{Falsification â€” runaway growth at $\Omega_{\rm eff}=1$:}
For $\beta=0.8$, the gravity term $\propto a^{-3.4}$ grows much faster than
the friction term $\propto a^{-2}$ at early times.  There is no valid
power-law matter-dominated era; the ODE solver (DOP853) fails with
step-size exhaustion.  PM with $\Omega_{\rm eff}=1$ and $\beta=0.8$
predicts catastrophic exponential over-growth â€” \emph{excluded}.
